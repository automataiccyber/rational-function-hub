Rational Functions

A rational function is a type of function you get when you divide one polynomial by another. It looks like this: f(x) = P(x) / Q(x), where P(x) and Q(x) are polynomials, and Q(x) â‰  0.

One important thing about rational functions is vertical asymptotes. These happen at values of x that make the bottom polynomial, Q(x), equal to zero. When this happens, the function is undefined and can go to infinity.

Another feature is horizontal asymptotes. These lines show what the function looks like as x gets really big or really small, helping to understand its long-term behavior.

Rational functions are used in many fields like physics, engineering, and economics. For example, they can help calculate speeds or concentrations of substances in chemistry.

Here are some key points about rational functions:

  • Discontinuities: They can have gaps or vertical asymptotes where they aren't defined.
  • Real-life uses: They are used to calculate things like speed and concentration.
  • Curve fitting: They help fit curves to data that changes in a hyperbolic way.
  • Control systems: Used in engineering to design systems that need precise control.

Learning about rational functions is useful because they help us understand and solve real-world problems. They also build a foundation for more advanced math concepts.

These functions are also studied in calculus, especially when learning about limits and integrals. This helps us understand continuous and discontinuous behavior in mathematical functions.
